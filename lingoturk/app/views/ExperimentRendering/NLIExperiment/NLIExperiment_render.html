Instructions

The goal

The goal of this experiment is to create a system that can automatically detect contradicting information across texts. We hope that some day this will help people navigate through the information wilderness that is the Internet. Not by telling them which information is true and which is fake, but by providing them with (other) sources that contradict or confirm some given statement. That way, people can make their own decision on which information seems trustworthy.

An example of a topic with a lot of conflicting views is vaccination. Therefore, the vaccination debate is our use case.
Can you help us determine which statements about vaccines are confirmed or denied in a given sentence?

The task

You will be presented with:

  1. a full SENTENCE, optionally quoting some PEOPLE/SOURCES
  2. an independent STATEMENT

We use the alias JOHN to refer to the AUTHOR of the sentence. Please indicate whether JOHN would AGREE or DISAGREE with the statement.

If there are other PEOPLE/SOURCES quoted in the sentence, please indicate their opinion with respect to the statement as well.

Some more detailed instructions and examples can be found on the next page.

Instructions (click to expand)

The labels

AGREEMENT If John beliefs what is in the sentence, he must believe that the statement is true.
DISAGREEMENT If John beliefs what is in the sentence, he must believe that the statement is false.
PARTIAL AGREEMENT If John beliefs what is in the sentence, he must believe that the statement is only partially true.
I CANNOT TELL The statement is about something else than the sentence, or it cannot be determined what John believes to be true.

The same or different topics?

JOHN WROTE: STATEMENT
I am convinced that vaccines are 100% safe. vaccines cause dangerous side effects
I am convinced that vaccines are 100% safe. vaccines are effective

From the sentence above, we can infer that John would disagree with the first statement, because "vaccines are 100% safe" and "vaccines cause dangerous side effects" cannot be true at the same time.

However, we CANNOT infer that John agrees with the second statement. After all, the safety and effectiveness of vaccines are two different topics, so John could believe that vaccines are safe but ineffective at the same time.

This might be confusing, because you may infer that John is pro-vaccine and therefore probably also beliefs that vaccines are effective. However, try not to guess based on what you know about pro- and anti-vaccine beliefs, but base your question only on what is written in the text.
